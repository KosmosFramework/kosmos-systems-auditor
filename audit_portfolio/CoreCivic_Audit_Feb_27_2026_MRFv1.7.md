## Phase 1: Seven Element Structure (7ES) analysis

### Inputs reveal total dependency on coercive state power

CoreCivic's input architecture confirms a system entirely parasitic on government authority. **One hundred percent of the company's $2.2 billion in 2025 revenue** derives from government contracts, with federal customers (ICE, USMS, BOP) accounting for 51% and state customers for 40%. ICE alone generated $744 million in 2025, nearly doubling its contribution from the prior year. The human inputs — approximately **65,000+ incarcerated individuals and detainees** occupying beds at 42 safety facilities, 21 community centers, and 6 leased properties across 19 states — arrive through coercive legal processes in which CoreCivic exercises no legitimate authority but from which it extracts maximum financial value.

The labor input pipeline is equally extractive. CoreCivic employs approximately 11,649 workers, but pays correctional officers as little as **$9 per hour** in a non-unionized environment, producing the 188% annual turnover rate documented at Trousdale Turner. Simultaneously, detainees perform facility maintenance — cleaning bathrooms, cafeterias, kitchens, and offices — for **$1 to $4 per day** under what courts have characterized as coercive conditions, including threats of solitary confinement for refusal. Multiple class actions under the Trafficking Victims Protection Act remain active, with the Owino v. CoreCivic case (S.D. Cal.) now in discovery phase with certified classes of thousands.

Political capital functions as a critical input. CoreCivic has spent approximately **$26.1 million on federal lobbying since 1998**, plus $3.7 million on Tennessee state political activities since 2009. Through its decades-long executive membership in ALEC's Public Safety and Elections Task Force, the company helped draft truth-in-sentencing laws, three-strikes legislation, and the Private Correctional Facilities Act — model bills that directly expanded its addressable market.

### Outputs optimize for extraction, not rehabilitation

The primary output — detention services measured in occupied bed-days — generates shareholder returns while producing measurably worse outcomes than public alternatives. The most rigorous academic evidence shows private prison incarceration **increases recidivism by 13–22%** (Minnesota, Oklahoma, and Florida studies), extends sentences by **60–90 additional days** (Mississippi study of 27,000 prisoners), and increases incarceration rates by **178 new prisoners per million population per year** when private prisons enter a state (Washington State University, 2020).

Secondary outputs include significant externalized costs. Tennessee's CoreCivic-operated prisons have recorded **300+ deaths since 2016**, with CBS News analysis showing inmates are twice as likely to be killed in CoreCivic facilities compared to state-run prisons. The Citrus County Detention Facility recorded a mortality rate of **426 per 100,000** — 2.5 times the national jail average. These deaths, alongside documented family separation, community trauma, and the generation of recidivism, represent waste products that the system makes no attempt to recapture or remediate.

Financial outputs flow unidirectionally toward shareholders and executives. Net income grew **69% to $116.5 million** in 2025, and the company repurchased **11.2 million shares for $218.4 million** while simultaneously operating facilities where a single officer oversees 360 prisoners. Former CEO Hininger's total compensation averaged approximately $7.47 million annually, with 85.4% in bonuses and equity. The board increased share repurchase authorization to $700 million in November 2025.

### Processing, controls, and feedback reveal systematic failure modes

CoreCivic's processing function is optimized for cost reduction, not human outcomes. The DOJ's 2016 Office of Inspector General report found contract prisons had **28% more inmate-on-inmate violence** and **twice as many inmate-on-staff assaults** as comparable BOP institutions. Idaho's Department of Correction documented prisoner-on-prisoner assaults at four times the rate of all other state prisons combined. Bureau of Justice Assistance data shows prisoner-on-prisoner assault rates **66% higher** and prisoner-on-staff assaults **49% higher** in private facilities.

The control architecture exhibits classic regulatory capture. CoreCivic's contracts with government agencies create a dual-loyalty problem: the regulators (government corrections departments) are simultaneously the company's customers. Tennessee demonstrates this dynamic most clearly — the state has fined CoreCivic **$29.5 million since 2022** for staffing failures, yet increased the company's payout by $7 million in the most recent budget. Governor Lee called CoreCivic "a very important partner" two days after the DOJ announced its civil rights investigation into Trousdale Turner.

Feedback mechanisms are systematically suppressed. CoreCivic concealed triple-bunking practices at its Leavenworth facility by uninstalling third beds before ACA accreditation audits. ICE has denied members of Congress access to detention facilities for oversight. At the Torrance County Detention Facility, when DHS OIG recommended immediate removal of all detainees due to critical staffing shortages, ICE rejected the findings and accused its own watchdog of fabricating evidence. Senator Elizabeth Warren's 2020 report found that ACA's private prison accreditation system is "riddled with conflicts of interest, lacks transparency, and is subject to zero accountability."

### Interface and environment: the political-financial ecosystem

CoreCivic's interface with the justice system operates through approximately 70 facilities holding contracts with ICE, USMS, BOP, and state corrections departments, with a 96% contract renewal rate over five years. The environmental context — mass incarceration policy, immigration enforcement, mandatory minimums, three-strikes laws — represents the political ecosystem CoreCivic helped construct through ALEC and continues to sustain through $1.97 million in federal lobbying in 2025 alone.

The company's own SEC filings acknowledge this dependency with remarkable candor: "The demand for our facilities and services could be adversely affected by the relaxation of enforcement efforts, leniency in conviction or parole standards and sentencing practices or through the decriminalization" of certain activities. This is a system that openly admits it needs punitive policy to survive.

## Phase 2: Fundamental Design Principles — all eight below viability threshold

### Symbiotic Purpose (SP): 0.4/10

*SP = 10 × (Benefits to all stakeholders / Benefits to controllers)*

The formula quantifies the ratio of mutual benefit to unilateral extraction. CoreCivic's controller class — shareholders receiving $116.5 million in net income and $218.4 million in buybacks (2025), and executives averaging $7.47 million in annual compensation — captures virtually all system benefits. The stakeholder class bearing costs — 65,000+ incarcerated individuals experiencing documented abuse, medical neglect, forced labor at $1/day, and death rates double those of public facilities — receives negative value. This score decreased from the surface audit's 0.5 to **0.4** based on the 2025 pay-to-play evidence.

### Adaptive Resilience (AR): 1.8/10

*AR = 10 × (1 − External interventions / Autonomous processes)*

Natural systems self-correct through internal feedback. CoreCivic demonstrates the opposite: identical problems documented in Tennessee state audits in **2017, 2020, and 2023** persist unchanged. The $29.5 million in state penalties failed to produce staffing improvements. The 188% guard turnover rate at Trousdale Turner reflects a system incapable of retaining its own workforce. Rather than adapting through better outcomes, the company adapts by shifting revenue sources — from BOP contracts (eliminated under Biden) to ICE contracts (exploding under Trump). This is market opportunism, not adaptive resilience. The company's survival depends on **100% external contract renewal**, making it among the least autonomously resilient systems analyzable. Score decreased from 2.0 to **1.8**.

### Reciprocal Ethics (RE): 1.0/10

*RE = 10 × (Fair exchanges / Total exchanges)*

In viable natural systems, exchanges create value for both parties. CoreCivic's exchanges are profoundly asymmetric. Detainees perform facility labor for $1–$4/day — work that would otherwise require paid employees — under conditions courts have found coercive. Academic evidence shows private prison incarceration increases recidivism by 13–22%, meaning the exchange actually worsens the outcome it purports to address. Score decreased from 1.2 to **1.0**.

### Closed-Loop Materiality (CLM): 0.8/10

*CLM = 10 × (Recycled outputs / Total outputs)*

Natural systems close resource loops — waste from one process becomes input for another. CoreCivic operates a purely linear extraction model. Human beings enter as "inputs," generate per-diem revenue, and exit with higher recidivism rates, more trauma, and fewer prospects. The Mississippi study documented prisoners serving **60–90 extra days** in private facilities with no reduction in reoffending. This means the system's primary material output (human beings post-incarceration) is returned to communities in worse condition than it received them, requiring additional system resources upon re-arrest. The DOJ found that "rehabilitative services that the Bureau provides, such as educational programs and job training, have proved difficult to replicate and outsource" in private settings. Zero recycling of outputs. Score unchanged at **0.8**.

CoreCivic concentrates authority in corporate headquarters (Brentwood, Tennessee) while systematically eliminating agency from every other system participant. Incarcerated individuals exercise zero agency over their conditions, medical care, food, or labor. Workers — non-unionized and paid as little as $9/hour — have limited ability to effect change (the wrongful termination of nurse Jurumay Oliva for reporting understaffing illustrates this). Communities where facilities are located exercise minimal voice: California City's mayor had no advance notice when ICE began transferring detainees; the Leavenworth city commission unanimously opposed reopening but was overridden through federal preemption. Even Congress has been denied facility access by ICE. Three firms (CoreCivic, GEO, MTC) control **96% of all private prison beds**, creating an effective oligopoly that further concentrates market power. Score decreased from 1.5 to **1.3**.

### Contextual Harmony (CH): 0.3/10

## Phase 5: Systemic FDP violations and biomimetic repair templates

### Violation pattern: the inverted symbiosis trap

CoreCivic exhibits what the KOSMOS framework identifies as **inverted symbiosis** — a relationship marketed as mutualistic (public safety through efficient incarceration) that is structurally parasitic (extraction of public funds while producing worse outcomes). Every measurable outcome contradicts the stated purpose. The academic evidence is unambiguous across multiple independent studies spanning decades: private prisons produce **higher recidivism** (13–22% increase across four major studies), **more violence** (28–66% higher assault rates), **longer sentences** (60–90 additional days), and **no meaningful cost savings** (meta-analyses find approximately 2.2%, not statistically significant when properly controlled).

The occupancy guarantee mechanism illustrates the inversion most precisely. When 65% of contracts require states to fill 80–100% of beds or pay for empty ones, the system creates a **direct financial penalty for reducing incarceration**. Arizona paid $3 million in settlement for failing to meet a 97% occupancy quota. Colorado paid $2 million in penalties despite significant crime reductions. CoreCivic offered in 2012 to purchase prisons from 48 states in exchange for 20-year contracts with 90% guaranteed occupancy. This is not a system that benefits from solving the problem it claims to address — it is a system that profits from the problem's perpetuation.

### Biomimetic repair template 1: from parasitic to mutualistic architecture

Natural mutualistic systems — mycorrhizal networks linking forest trees, cleaner fish stations on coral reefs — succeed because **both parties derive non-extractable benefit** from the relationship. A biomimetic redesign of CoreCivic's function would require:

- **Outcome-based contracts** replacing per-diem payment. Revenue tied to verified reductions in recidivism (measured at 3- and 5-year horizons), successful community reintegration metrics, and participant wellbeing indicators. No outcomes-based contracts currently exist in CoreCivic's portfolio.
- **Elimination of occupancy guarantees.** Contracts structured so the operator benefits financially from reducing its census through successful rehabilitation — the inverse of the current incentive.
- **Distributed governance.** Facility-level boards including incarcerated individuals, families, community members, workers, and independent oversight. This addresses the DA score of 1.3 by distributing decision-making across stakeholders.

### Biomimetic repair template 2: closing the material loop

Natural systems produce no waste — every output becomes input for another process. CoreCivic's linear model takes human beings, degrades them (higher recidivism, more trauma, documented abuse), and returns them to communities in worse condition. A closed-loop design would:

- **Treat education, healthcare, and skill development as core operational outputs**, measured and incentivized as rigorously as bed-day revenue. Minnesota's public prisons offered "greater variety of programming" and lower recidivism — demonstrating the viability of regenerative approaches.
- **Invest in community capacity** surrounding facilities rather than extracting from it. The Oklahoma Policy Institute's finding that private detention centers "hinder economic growth" reflects a system that degrades its own operating environment — antithetical to natural system design.
- **Eliminate forced labor below minimum wage.** Academic estimates suggest paying minimum wage would reduce private prison profits by 25%, but would align the labor exchange with Reciprocal Ethics principles and eliminate TVPA legal exposure.

### Biomimetic repair template 3: restoring feedback integrity

Natural systems that suppress feedback signals (pain response, immune function, predator detection) rapidly collapse. CoreCivic's systematic feedback suppression — falsifying staffing records, concealing triple-bunking, retaliating against whistleblowers, blocking Congressional oversight — mirrors autoimmune dysfunction in biological systems. Repair requires:

- **Mandatory real-time public reporting** of staffing levels, incident rates, medical response times, and death investigations — addressing the ET score of 0.0 through radical transparency.
- **Independent, unannounced inspection authority** with the power to immediately suspend operations. The Federal Prison Oversight Act (2024) provides a legislative template, but current ACA accreditation — with documented conflicts of interest — fails this function entirely.
- **Whistleblower protection** with financial incentives. Nurse Jurumay Oliva's termination for reporting that 2 nurses served 1,500 detainees at Otay Mesa represents a system that punishes error correction rather than rewarding it.
