# KOSMOS Systemic Policy Audit: H.R. 7567, the Farm, Food, and National Security Act of 2026

**H.R. 7567 is a structurally unnatural legislative system that concentrates benefits among the largest agricultural operations and corporate interests while imposing cumulative harm on 40 million SNAP recipients, small farmers, farmworkers, and rural communities.** This 802-page bill, introduced February 13, 2026, by House Agriculture Committee Chair Glenn Thompson (R-PA), functions as a "companion piece" to the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21), which already enacted $187 billion in SNAP cuts[1][2], expanded commodity subsidies, and enhanced crop insurance. Combined, these two instruments represent the most consequential restructuring of American agricultural policy since the 1996 welfare reform — redistributing roughly $220 billion away from nutrition assistance toward commodity producers and corporate interests over the next decade. The bill's Global FDP score of 2.00/10 classifies it as an Unnatural System requiring fundamental redesign rather than incremental reform.

## Phase 1: The Seven-Element Structural Dissection Reveals Brittle Architecture

### Input Analysis

The system draws from three primary input streams. Federal funding constitutes the dominant input: the CBO baseline projects $1.4 trillion in mandatory farm bill spending over FY2027-2036, with SNAP at $985 billion (70%), crop insurance at $156 billion (11%), and commodity programs and conservation comprising the remainder[3][4]. Political capital serves as the critical shadow input — the bill was drafted with heavy lobbying from the American Farm Bureau Federation, National Pork Producers Council, Bayer/Monsanto (whose Agricultural Labeling Uniformity Act represents their top legislative priority), and the crop insurance industry[5][6]. Agricultural production capacity (~900 million acres of farmland) and farmer labor constitute the physical inputs.

The most consequential hidden input is the reconciliation bill itself (P.L. 119-21). H.R. 7567 cannot be understood as a standalone system — it was deliberately designed to complement reconciliation provisions that handled the largest budgetary items[7][8]. This fragmented architecture obscures total system impacts: a citizen reading H.R. 7567 alone would not understand that SNAP has already been cut by $187 billion. This structural obfuscation is a critical brittleness point because it prevents meaningful public feedback on the combined policy architecture.

### Output Analysis

Outputs divide sharply between beneficiaries and cost-bearers. Primary beneficiaries include the top 10% of farm operations (receiving 78% of commodity subsidies)[9][10], pesticide manufacturers (gaining federal preemption of state regulations and lawsuit immunity)[11], the crop insurance industry ($156 billion in projected outlays), and large commodity producers receiving reference price increases of 10-20%. Primary cost-bearers include 40 million SNAP recipients facing cumulative benefit reductions (17 million children, 6 million older adults, 4 million people with disabilities)[12][13], farmworkers exposed to weakened pesticide protections, state and local governments losing regulatory authority, and small/mid-size farmers losing access to cancelled local food programs worth over $1 billion[14].

### Processing, Controls, and Feedback

Processing occurs through USDA administrative implementation, state-level program administration, and Farm Service Agency/NRCS field delivery. This processing layer is critically degraded: NRCS has lost 22% of its workforce (2,673 employees) in the first half of 2025 alone, undermining conservation program delivery capacity[15]. Controls include Congressional oversight, CBO scoring, and USDA rulemaking — but the bill weakens several control mechanisms by making dietary guideline updates optional (extending the review cycle from 5 to potentially 10 years)[16], restricting USDA's CCC spending authority, and preempting state/local regulatory controls over pesticides[17].

Feedback loops are the system's most dysfunctional element. The bill mandates GAO investigation of SNAP administrative costs and expands error reporting — but these measure program efficiency, not effectiveness. There is no mechanism for SNAP recipients, farmworkers, or rural communities to provide feedback on program adequacy. The dietary guidelines process — the most direct feedback loop between nutrition science and benefit levels — is weakened by making updates discretionary and less frequent. CBO's 10-year scoring window systematically underestimates long-term impacts, particularly for provisions like the Thrifty Food Plan restriction whose costs compound over time[18].

### Interface and Environment Failures

The federal-state interface is destabilized by the reconciliation bill's unprecedented requirement that states share SNAP benefit costs. CBO estimates this may eliminate benefits for 300,000 people per month and could cause states to withdraw from SNAP entirely[19][20]. The USDA-farmer interface is degraded by the NRCS staffing crisis. The legislative-lobbyist interface is highly permeable — 90% of Democratic provisions were rejected, while Bayer's pesticide immunity provision and the NPPC's Proposition 12 override were included wholesale[21]. The bill operates in an environment of agricultural trade disruption (tariffs), increasing climate volatility (drought, flooding), partisan polarization, and institutional capacity erosion at USDA — yet addresses none of these environmental stressors structurally.

## Phase 2: Fundamental Design Principles Reveal Systemic Extraction

### FDP Scoring with Domain-Specific Weights for Legislative Policy

Each FDP is scored 0-10 using the KOSMOS biomimetic formulas, with domain-specific weights applied for socioeconomic-policy systems (SP and RE weighted 3×; ET and IH weighted 2×; AR, DA, CH, CLM weighted 1×).

**FDP 1 — Symbiotic Purpose: 2.5/10** (Mandatory cap applied). The SP formula measures benefits to all stakeholders relative to benefits to controllers. The top 10% of farm operations collect 78% of commodity payments. The bottom 80% receive 9%[22]. Nearly 10,000 recipients have collected subsidies for 40 consecutive years, totaling $10.7 billion[23]. Meanwhile, 40 million SNAP participants face benefit cuts — exceeding the 10% population threshold that triggers the MRF mandatory SP cap of ≤3. The bill's title invokes "National Security," but the primary beneficiaries are large commodity producers and Bayer/Monsanto, not national food security broadly. Natural benchmark: Bee pollination networks distribute benefits across all participants. This system concentrates nectar among the largest flowers while starving the smaller ones.

**FDP 2 — Adaptive Resilience: 2.0/10.** The AR formula measures self-correction capacity without external enforcement. The farm bill system has zero autonomous self-correction. Without Congressional reauthorization (which has failed three consecutive times since 2023, requiring emergency extensions)[24], programs revert to Depression-era permanent law. Without annual appropriations, discretionary programs cease immediately. Without USDA staff implementation, conservation programs cannot be delivered. The NRCS staffing crisis (22% loss) demonstrates that the system cannot even maintain its existing processing capacity, let alone self-correct. The bill's Marketing Assistance Loan provision specifically addresses the fact that programs collapse during government shutdowns — an admission of intrinsic fragility[25].

**FDP 3 — Reciprocal Ethics: 2.0/10.** The RE formula measures the ratio of fair exchanges to total exchanges. The bill contains multiple extractive exchanges: SNAP recipients lose ~$30 billion in future benefits while commodity producers gain expanded subsidies[26]. Bayer receives federal immunity from 125,000+ cancer lawsuits while cancer victims lose legal recourse[27]. State and local governments lose pesticide regulatory authority while receiving nothing in return. California voters' Proposition 12 (approved by 63% of voters) is nullified[28]. Only a few exchanges approach reciprocity: expanded credit access for beginning farmers, rural broadband improvements, and EBT card security enhancement.

**FDP 4 — Closed-Loop Materiality: 3.0/10.** Agricultural policy inherently involves material flows — soil, water, nutrients, food. The bill's conservation provisions (CRP reauthorization, precision agriculture support, biochar research, watershed rehabilitation) represent partial closed-loop thinking[29]. However, the CRP is capped at 27 million acres (advocates sought 40 million)[30], conservation programs have lost their IRA climate funding, and payment limits for EQIP/CSP were removed by reconciliation — allowing the largest operations to consume disproportionate conservation dollars[31]. The solar farmland restriction actively blocks renewable energy integration into agricultural landscapes[32]. No provisions address the $26 billion annual cost of agricultural water pollution or soil erosion externalities.

**FDP 5 — Distributed Agency: 3.0/10.** The DA formula measures decentralization of decision-making. The bill was drafted with 90% exclusion of minority party provisions — an unprecedented centralization for farm bill legislation, which historically achieves bipartisan passage (the 2018 farm bill passed 369-47)[33]. The pesticide preemption and Proposition 12 override eliminate distributed agency by removing state/local regulatory power. However, some distributed elements exist: state SNAP administration (weakened by cost-sharing mandates), farmer choice in CRP enrollment, county-level participation in forestry categorical exclusions, and the local food procurement program design. The bill's GovTrack passage estimate of 11% reflects the political unsustainability of hyper-centralized drafting[34].

**FDP 6 — Contextual Harmony: 3.5/10.** The CH formula measures enhancement of local contexts. Positive local impacts include rural broadband expansion (raising minimum speeds from 25/3 to 50/25 Mbps), rural childcare prioritization, hospital technical assistance codification, and watershed rehabilitation cost-sharing increases (65% to 90%)[35]. Negative local impacts include pesticide preemption overriding protections near schools, parks, and playgrounds in 40+ states[36]; SNAP cuts reducing food purchasing power in local economies; cancellation of Local Food Purchase Assistance programs ($1 billion in lost local farm revenue); and the feral swine program's allocation of only 40% to on-farm assistance versus 60% to federal APHIS. The bill's failure to address climate adaptation leaves rural communities exposed to increasing drought, flooding, and extreme weather.

**FDP 7 — Emergent Transparency: 3.0/10.** The ET formula includes a 2× penalty for withheld data. The bill's 802 pages of statutory language, cross-references to existing law, and dependence on understanding the reconciliation bill create substantial opacity[37]. The drafting process excluded minority party input (90% rejection rate). The "Agricultural Labeling Uniformity Act" title conceals the provision's primary function (pesticide manufacturer lawsuit immunity)[38]. The TFP restriction is framed as "reform" rather than the largest SNAP benefit cut in 30 years. Positive transparency elements include CBO scoring, the EWG farm subsidy database (enabling public tracking of payment recipients)[39], and mandated GAO investigations. However, approximately 20% of required audit data is obscured by the fragmented two-bill architecture, triggering the MRF's withheld data penalty.

**FDP 8 — Intellectual Honesty: 2.0/10.** The IH formula measures hidden versus acknowledged trade-offs. The bill's title ("Farm, Food, and National Security") implies comprehensive benefit, yet the bill's primary beneficiaries are large commodity operations and pesticide companies. The trade-off between SNAP cuts and commodity subsidy increases is never acknowledged in the bill text. The pesticide provision is titled "labeling uniformity" rather than "manufacturer immunity." The local food program offers $200 million in discretionary authorization to replace $1 billion+ in cancelled mandatory programs — described by advocates as "an empty promise"[40]. The bill claims to address "national security" but contains no provisions addressing agricultural vulnerability to trade disruption, climate volatility, or supply chain concentration. The 2018 farm bill's bipartisan tradition is invoked while the drafting process is exclusively partisan.

## Phase 3: Designer Genealogy and Collapse Prognosis

### Designer Query Discriminator Classifies the System as Unnatural

**Designer Traceability (DT): 0.85/1.0.** The system is explicitly designed by identifiable actors. Rep. Thompson and House Agriculture Committee staff drafted the bill with documented input from Bayer/Monsanto (whose "Modern Ag Alliance" lobbied for Section 10205), the National Pork Producers Council (~1,000 agricultural groups supporting the Prop 12 override), the American Farm Bureau Federation (dairy provisions "developed with direct input from Farm Bureau"), and the crop insurance industry[41][42]. The revolving door between USDA, Congress, and agribusiness is extensive. Campaign contribution patterns from agricultural interests to Agriculture Committee members are well-documented. The design fingerprints are unmistakable.

**Goal Alignment (GA): 0.25/1.0.** Goals are poorly aligned with ecological flourishing and broad human welfare. The bill optimizes for large-scale commodity production and corporate profit protection rather than regenerative agriculture, equitable food access, or environmental health. Conservation provisions exist but are underfunded, understaffed, and structurally subordinate to commodity title spending. The pesticide preemption actively undermines ecological alignment. The solar farmland restriction opposes renewable energy transition[43]. The SNAP cuts reduce food security for 40 million people. Some alignment exists through biochar research, soil health provisions, and watershed programs, but these represent a small fraction of the bill's impact.

**Enforcement Dependency (ED): 0.85/1.0.** The system is entirely dependent on external enforcement. Without Congressional reauthorization (already failed three times), programs revert to chaotic Depression-era law. Without annual appropriations, discretionary programs cease. Without USDA staffing (already 22% depleted at NRCS), conservation programs cannot be delivered. Without state compliance with cost-sharing mandates, SNAP administration collapses. Without CBO scoring and GAO oversight, there is no accountability mechanism. The Marketing Assistance Loan shutdown fix in the bill is itself evidence that the system cannot survive lapses in enforcement.

**DQD Composite: (0.85 + 0.25 + 0.85) / 3 = 0.65**

**Classification: UNNATURAL (>0.60).** The farm bill system is deliberately designed by identifiable actors with extractive agendas, poorly aligned with ecological flourishing, and entirely dependent on external enforcement. This classification distinguishes it from natural systems (emergent, self-sustaining) and hybrid systems (partially designed but incorporating natural principles). The DQD of 0.65 places it at the threshold of Unnatural classification — reflecting the genuine value in some bill components (conservation, rural development, beginning farmer support) that partially moderate the overall extractive design.

### Observer's Collapse Function Identifies Moderate Instability

**Recursive Belief Factor (B_R): 0.70.** The farm bill system requires ongoing political belief to persist. The bipartisan "farm bill coalition" — historically the most durable alliance in Congress, linking rural farm interests with urban nutrition interests — is fracturing. Democrats refuse to support H.R. 7567 without reversing SNAP cuts[44]. The "national security" framing requires sustained public belief in American agricultural vulnerability. GovTrack's 11% enactment probability reflects eroding political belief in this particular system design. However, the abstract concept of farm legislation retains broad public support, moderating B_R below maximum levels.

**Observer Dependency (D_C): 0.75.** The system requires conscious participation at multiple levels: Congressional reauthorization votes, annual appropriations decisions, USDA administrative implementation, state-level SNAP administration, farmer program enrollment, and public tolerance of the subsidy structure. The reconciliation bill's state cost-sharing requirement added a new dependency layer — states must now actively choose to fund SNAP rather than passively administering federal benefits[45]. States' withdrawal constitutes a collapse vector that did not exist before 2025.

**Intrinsic Stability (T_S): 1.20.** The farm bill system possesses significant institutional momentum from 90+ years of operation (since the Agricultural Adjustment Act of 1933). USDA infrastructure, the Farm Service Agency, and state extension services provide structural stability. Agricultural production continues regardless of policy. However, the current system's stability is degraded by USDA staffing cuts, the unprecedented partisan approach, the fragmented two-bill architecture, and the September 30, 2026 expiration deadline. T_S = 1.20 reflects moderate institutional resilience.

**OCF Composite: (0.70 × 0.75) / 1.20 = 0.44**

**Classification: MODERATE INSTABILITY (0.30-0.60).** The farm bill system exists in a zone of significant but not imminent collapse risk. It is sustained more by institutional tradition and inertia than by broad observer belief in its current design. The collapse trigger analysis identifies several plausible cascade scenarios: if states begin withdrawing from SNAP administration (cost-sharing pressure), if the bill fails markup (scheduled February 23), if the Senate refuses to act before the September 2026 expiration, or if the farm coalition permanently fractures along partisan lines. The 25% participation-loss cascade threshold (per Centola network effects research) is approaching — if Democratic refusal to support the bill hardens and fiscal conservatives defect, the coalition falls below critical mass.

## Phase 4: Counterfactual and Adversarial Readings by Major Section

### Title I (Commodities) — "Farmers Need These Safety Nets"

**Strongest defense**: American agriculture faces genuine risks from weather volatility, global trade disruption, and input cost inflation. Commodity programs provide a floor that prevents farm bankruptcies and ensures stable food production. The MAL shutdown fix addresses a real problem that harmed farmers during the fall 2025 government shutdown[46].

**Structural counter**: The safety net is a trampoline for the wealthy. EWG and Heritage Foundation jointly note that the top 10% of farm operations collect 78% of commodity payments[47]. Average farm household income for subsidy recipients exceeds $145,000 — well above the national median. Nearly 80,000 "city slickers" in major metro areas collected $2 billion in farm subsidies between 2020-2024, and 44 of the 9,526 repeat payment recipients do not work or live on farms[48]. Reference price increases of 10-20% (enacted via reconciliation) ensure most cotton, rice, and peanut farmers receive payments every year, converting a safety net into a permanent income transfer. The bill perpetuates this structure without reform.

**Counterfactual**: A regenerative commodity system (FDP target: 7.0) would tie payments to farm size (graduated caps), require conservation compliance, prioritize beginning and socially disadvantaged farmers, and phase out payments as farm income rises above regional medians. Such a system would distribute the same funding more equitably while encouraging ecological stewardship.

### Title IV (Nutrition) — "SNAP Reform Improves Program Integrity"

**Strongest defense**: The bill makes SNAP online purchasing permanent, requires chip-enabled EBT cards to combat skimming fraud, and restores eligibility for people returning from prison. The GAO investigation of administrative costs promotes accountability. The GusNIP reauthorization supports fruit and vegetable purchases[49].

**Structural counter**: These improvements are cosmetic bandages on a hemorrhaging system. The bill adds ~$30 billion in SNAP benefit cuts through TFP restrictions while failing to reverse $187 billion in reconciliation cuts[50]. Combined, these represent the largest SNAP reductions in history, affecting 40 million people — including 17 million children. Arizona already reported "alarming declines" in SNAP participation between July and December 2025 after reconciliation cuts took effect. The GusNIP reauthorization includes no funding increase. The local food program replaces $1 billion+ in cancelled mandatory programs with $200 million in discretionary authorization — what NSAC called "an empty promise" since annual appropriations are unreliable[51]. The TFP restriction prevents USDA from adjusting benefits to reflect actual food costs, dietary science, or consumption patterns, freezing benefits at increasingly inadequate levels in perpetuity.

**Counterfactual**: A symbiotic nutrition system (SP target: 8.0) would restore pre-reconciliation SNAP benefit levels, mandate the TFP reevaluation every 5 years using current nutrition science, provide mandatory funding for local food programs connecting small farms to schools and food banks, and extend full SNAP benefits to Puerto Rico. Such a system would reduce food insecurity below 10% within 5 years.

### Title X (Horticulture/Marketing) — "Federal Uniformity Protects Interstate Commerce"

**Strongest defense**: The pesticide labeling provisions create nationwide uniformity, reducing compliance costs for agricultural producers who ship across state lines. The Proposition 12 override prevents a patchwork of state animal housing standards that could increase consumer prices. Both provisions facilitate efficient interstate commerce under the Commerce Clause[52].

**Structural counter**: "Uniformity" is a euphemism for corporate immunity. Section 10205 was drafted by Bayer/Monsanto's lobbying apparatus to shield the company from 125,000+ Roundup cancer lawsuits that have cost over $10 billion[53]. The provision would preempt protections in 40+ states governing pesticide use near schools, parks, and playgrounds[54]. EWG's Geoff Horsfield noted that House Republicans "can't credibly claim to back an agenda that supports public health or protects kids" while advancing this provision[55]. The Proposition 12 override nullifies a law approved by 63% of California voters and supported by some independent hog farmers who gained rare market access under that law[56]. The beneficiaries are meatpackers and large-scale confined animal operations, not consumers or independent farmers. The "Make America Healthy Again" movement — allied with the bill's own party — opposes the pesticide provision.

**Counterfactual**: A contextually harmonious regulatory system (CH target: 8.5) would maintain federal minimum standards while allowing states to impose stricter protections based on local conditions. It would require pesticide manufacturers to disclose all known health risks and would fund independent epidemiological research on agricultural chemical exposure. Such a system would protect both interstate commerce and local populations.

### Title IX (Energy) — "Protecting Prime Farmland from Solar Development"

**Strongest defense**: Converting productive agricultural land to solar installations reduces domestic food production capacity. The bill's restrictions protect prime farmland while allowing small-scale and on-farm solar projects. The joint USDA/DOE study will provide data-driven guidance for future policy[57].

**Structural counter**: The solar restriction is agricultural protectionism masquerading as food security. Solar-farmland coexistence ("agrivoltaics") is proven to increase crop yields for shade-tolerant crops while generating clean energy. The 5-acre and 50-acre thresholds are arbitrary and impede rural economic diversification. Meanwhile, the bill provides $150 million for feral swine eradication but no comparable investment in renewable energy transition for rural communities[58]. The omission of year-round E15 sales — expected by bipartisan ethanol-state lawmakers — further reveals that the energy title prioritizes fossil-fuel-aligned agricultural interests over genuine energy security[59].

**Counterfactual**: A closed-loop energy system (CLM target: 7.5) would incentivize agrivoltaic installations that combine food production with energy generation, fund rural community renewable energy cooperatives, mandate year-round E15 sales, and invest in biochar production infrastructure. This would enhance both food security and energy security while generating rural income.

### Title II (Conservation) — "Voluntary, Incentive-Based Conservation Works"

**Strongest defense**: The bill reauthorizes CRP through 2031, increases watershed rehabilitation cost-sharing from 65% to 90%, funds precision agriculture for conservation purposes, and advances the feral swine eradication program. These voluntary, incentive-based approaches are more effective than regulatory mandates[60].

**Structural counter**: Conservation spending is the bill's strongest component, yet it remains structurally inadequate. The CRP cap remains at 27 million acres (advocates sought 40 million)[61]. NRCS has lost 22% of its workforce, undermining program delivery. The reconciliation bill removed payment limits for EQIP and CSP, allowing "very large operations to consume disproportionate amounts of conservation dollars" (per NSAC)[62]. IRA conservation climate funding was rescinded. Precision agriculture primarily benefits large, capital-intensive operations over diversified, small-scale farming. The absence of conservation compliance requirements for commodity programs decouples production incentives from environmental stewardship.

**Counterfactual**: An adaptive conservation system (AR target: 7.0) would expand CRP to 40 million acres, restore NRCS staffing, tie commodity payments to conservation compliance, impose graduated payment limits on EQIP/CSP, prioritize climate-smart practices, and fund farmer-led conservation research. Such a system would self-correct by linking economic incentives to environmental outcomes.

## Conclusion: The Farm Bill's Extractive Architecture Demands Structural Transformation

H.R. 7567 is not merely a flawed piece of legislation — it is a symptom of a systemically extractive agricultural policy architecture that has been optimized over decades to serve concentrated interests while using nutrition programs and rural development as political bargaining chips. The bill's Global FDP of 2.00/10 places it firmly in the Unnatural range, comparable to other KOSMOS-audited systems that require complete redesign rather than incremental repair.

Three structural features distinguish this bill's dysfunctions from ordinary legislative imperfection. First, the fragmented two-bill architecture (reconciliation + authorization) is itself an engineered opacity mechanism that prevents holistic public evaluation of policy impacts. Second, the destruction of farm bill bipartisanship — historically the cornerstone of farm legislation — eliminates the self-correcting feedback loop that moderated extreme positions for 90 years. Third, the pesticide immunity provision represents a qualitative threshold violation: using an agricultural authorization bill to shield a single corporation from 125,000+ cancer lawsuits is not farm policy but corporate capture of the legislative process.

The bill's OCF of 0.44 signals approaching instability rather than imminent collapse. The farm bill system retains institutional momentum (T_S = 1.20) from nine decades of operation. But the fracturing of the farm-nutrition coalition, state resistance to SNAP cost-sharing, USDA institutional degradation, and the September 2026 expiration deadline create converging collapse vectors. If observer belief continues withdrawing (GovTrack estimates only 11% enactment probability), the system may require yet another emergency extension — the fourth consecutive failure to complete farm bill reauthorization since 2023.

### Repair Recommendations Based on Biomimetic Redesign Principles

**Restore Symbiotic Purpose (SP target: 7.0)**: Reverse SNAP cuts from reconciliation, mandate TFP reevaluations every 5 years, provide mandatory funding for local food programs, extend full nutrition assistance to Puerto Rico, and graduate commodity payments inversely with farm size.

**Build Adaptive Resilience (AR target: 6.5)**: Restore NRCS staffing to pre-2025 levels, link commodity payments to conservation compliance, create automatic SNAP benefit adjusters tied to food price indices, and establish permanent reauthorization mechanisms that prevent reversion to Depression-era law.

**Enforce Reciprocal Ethics (RE target: 7.0)**: Remove the pesticide manufacturer immunity provision, maintain state regulatory authority over pesticides and animal welfare, impose graduated payment limits on all programs, and require independent cost-benefit analysis for all provisions exceeding $1 billion in 10-year impact.

**Increase Distributed Agency (DA target: 7.5)**: Restore bipartisan drafting process, expand state flexibility in conservation program design, strengthen tribal sovereignty over food programs, and create farmer advisory councils with binding input authority on commodity program design.

**Achieve Emergent Transparency (ET target: 8.0)**: Require unified CBO scoring of combined reconciliation and authorization impacts, mandate plain-language summaries of all provisions, publish lobbying contacts during drafting, and require disclosure of all entities receiving payments exceeding $100,000 annually.

A regenerative American agricultural system modeled on mycelial network principles — distributing resources to where they are needed most, recycling waste into nutrients, maintaining network resilience through redundancy, and operating transparently through chemical signaling — would achieve a Global FDP of approximately 7.5/10, a DQD below 0.40 (Natural classification), and an OCF below 0.25 (Stable). The gap between this natural benchmark and H.R. 7567's scores (Global FDP: 2.00, DQD: 0.65, OCF: 0.44) quantifies the magnitude of transformation required.

**Final Classification: UNNATURAL SYSTEM — MODERATE COLLAPSE RISK — STRUCTURAL REDESIGN REQUIRED**
